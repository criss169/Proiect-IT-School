Guide to Traditional Romanian Food

Mămăligă

Mămăligă is a traditional Romanian dish made from cornmeal. It has a porridge-like consistency and is usually served as a side dish with sour cream and grated cheese. It is often enjoyed alongside meat dishes or used as a base for various toppings and sauces.

Mămăligă is a staple in Romanian cuisine and has a long history, dating back to ancient times when corn was introduced to the region. It is a versatile dish that can be served in different ways, depending on the accompanying ingredients and flavors.

One popular variation is "bulz," where mămăligă is mixed with cheese and grilled. Another variation is "tochitură," which is a hearty dish made with mămăligă, pork, and a rich sauce.

Sarmale

Sarmale are cabbage rolls stuffed with a mixture of ground pork, rice, and spices. They are cooked in a savory tomato sauce and are a staple dish in Romanian cuisine, especially during festive occasions.

Sarmale are often served hot and are accompanied by sour cream and polenta. They are a beloved dish in Romania and represent the country's culinary heritage. The preparation of sarmale is a labor of love, often involving family gatherings and sharing of traditional recipes.

Sarmale variations can include using vine leaves instead of cabbage or adding different fillings such as beef, lamb, or vegetarian options. Each region of Romania has its own unique take on this classic dish.

Mici

Mici are grilled ground meat rolls made from a mixture of beef, lamb, and pork. They are seasoned with various spices such as garlic, thyme, and coriander.

Mici are popular street food in Romania and are often served with mustard, bread, and pickles. They are best enjoyed hot and are a favorite among locals and visitors alike. Mici are often a centerpiece at outdoor gatherings and are a symbol of Romanian culinary culture.

While mici are commonly associated with grilling, they can also be pan-fried or cooked in the oven. Each region has its own twist on the recipe, adding their own blend of spices and flavors.

Papanași

Papanași are fried doughnuts made with cottage cheese. They are typically served with sour cream and fruit preserves, such as cherry or raspberry.

Papanași are a beloved dessert in Romania and are known for their soft and fluffy texture. They are often enjoyed as a sweet treat or as a delightful ending to a hearty meal. Papanași are a popular choice during holidays and special occasions, and they reflect the country's love for indulgent and comforting desserts.

Traditionally, papanași are shaped into large dumplings and fried until golden brown. The doughnut-like exterior gives way to a creamy and tangy center. They are sometimes dusted with powdered sugar or drizzled with honey for added sweetness.

Cozonac

Cozonac is a sweet bread that is a traditional treat in Romania, especially during holidays and special occasions. It is made from a rich dough that is filled with a mixture of walnuts, cocoa, sugar, and sometimes raisins. The dough is carefully braided and baked until golden brown.

Cozonac is known for its soft and fluffy texture and its delightful combination of sweet flavors. It is often enjoyed with a cup of tea or coffee and is a symbol of celebration and togetherness in Romanian culture.

Cozonac variations can include using different fillings such as poppy seeds, nuts, or chocolate. It is a labor-intensive pastry that requires skill and patience in the preparation and shaping of the dough.

Zacusca

Zacusca is a traditional Romanian vegetable spread made from roasted eggplants, bell peppers, and tomatoes. It is a versatile dish that can be enjoyed as a spread on bread or as a flavorful accompaniment to various dishes.

The preparation of zacusca involves roasting the vegetables until they are soft and charred, which gives the spread a smoky flavor. The roasted vegetables are then peeled, chopped, and sautéed with onions and garlic, along with a combination of spices such as paprika, thyme, and bay leaves.

Zacusca is typically preserved in jars, allowing the flavors to develop and intensify over time. It is a popular homemade preserve that is made in large batches during the late summer and early autumn, when the vegetables are at their peak ripeness.

In Romanian cuisine, zacusca is a beloved accompaniment to meals and is often enjoyed during the winter months. It can be spread on fresh bread or used as a filling for sandwiches. Zacusca can also be served as a dip for crackers or vegetable sticks, adding a burst of flavor to appetizers.

Every family has its own recipe for zacusca, with slight variations in the combination of vegetables and spices used. It is a cherished culinary tradition that is passed down through generations, with each cook adding their own personal touch.

Ciorbă de Perişoare

Ciorbă de Perişoare is a traditional Romanian soup made with meatballs and sour broth. It is a comforting and flavorful dish that is often enjoyed as a main course or as a starter in Romanian cuisine.

To prepare Ciorbă de Perişoare, the meatballs are made from a mixture of ground meat, usually a combination of beef and pork, mixed with breadcrumbs, eggs, garlic, and spices such as parsley and dill. The meatballs are then cooked in a sour broth made from vegetables, sour cream, and lemon juice.

The sourness in the broth is achieved through the addition of fermented wheat bran or fermented barley bran, known as "bors." This gives the soup its distinct tangy flavor. The sourness can be adjusted to personal preference by adding more or less bors.

Ciorbă de Perişoare is often served hot and garnished with fresh herbs such as parsley or dill. It is commonly enjoyed with a dollop of sour cream on top, which adds a creamy richness to the soup. The soup is typically accompanied by freshly baked bread or traditional Romanian cornmeal porridge known as "mămăligă."

Like many traditional Romanian dishes, each family has its own recipe for Ciorbă de Perişoare, with variations in the choice of meat, seasonings, and the level of sourness in the broth. It is a beloved soup that brings warmth and comfort, particularly during cold winter days.

Romanian Stew

Romanian stew, also known as "tocană," is a classic dish that showcases the rich flavors and hearty ingredients of Romanian cuisine. It is a slow-cooked dish made with tender pieces of meat, vegetables, and aromatic spices.

The meat used in Romanian stew can vary, with popular choices including beef, pork, or a combination of both. The meat is typically cut into bite-sized cubes and browned to enhance its flavor before being simmered in a flavorful broth.

Along with the meat, Romanian stew incorporates a medley of vegetables such as onions, carrots, potatoes, bell peppers, and tomatoes. These vegetables add depth and texture to the stew, and their flavors meld together during the cooking process.

To enhance the taste of the stew, a variety of spices and herbs are added, including paprika, thyme, bay leaves, and garlic. These seasonings infuse the stew with a wonderful aroma and a distinctive Romanian flavor profile.

The stew is traditionally cooked slowly over low heat, allowing the flavors to develop and the meat to become tender. This slow cooking process results in a comforting and flavorful dish that is perfect for chilly days or when you're craving a hearty meal.

Romanian stew is often served with a side of crusty bread or mămăligă, which helps soak up the flavorful sauce. It is a dish that brings people together, and it is commonly enjoyed with family and friends during gatherings and celebrations.
